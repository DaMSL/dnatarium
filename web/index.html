Course Description

Big Data systems and analyses are enabling computer scientists to have an unprecedented impact on a wide variety of science, healthcare, economic and humanities applications.
By making datasets and their analyses the center of focus, big data principles and methods empowers a data-driven dialogue with domain experts, supported by powerful tools, abstractions and computing capabilities to collect, process, visualize and explore data.

This class provides a semester-long experiential learning opportunity on select big data systems and analyses, alongside an end-to-end application of these tools and methods on a concrete scientific dataset. This year, our application of choice is gene sequence analysis, with an aim of developing a public open science database as the culmination of student projects.

CS 600.615 is structured in three parts. First, we study the abstractions and systems design principles behind four popular big data processing frameworks (Hadoop, Dremel/Impala, GraphLab, Spark), including how these design principles facilitates their scalability. Next, the course syllabus covers an investigation of common workloads and use cases of these systems in both industrial and academic applications. These workloads are typically analytical in nature, and the course will then expose students to the scalable implementation of statistics and machine learning methods on these frameworks. Finally, students will apply the skills they have gained in developing an analysis stack for our science dataset through a semester-long course project.

CS 600.615 is a discussion- and project-oriented seminar course. Its format and workload includes weekly readings of academic conference papers, student presentations and in-class discussion on the assigned material, two short paper writing assignments, and a substantial project component. Students work in small groups of 2-3 on all course activities, including presentations, short papers and projects. This year, the course project will be structured as a concerted, class-wide effort to produce a tangible data and software artifact. Student groups may choose to work in one of three areas (see the Organization section), and will integrate their efforts into a single framework to be released by the semester's end.

Area: Systems.
Prereq: CS 600.315/415 or equivalent.

Organization

This course has a discussion-oriented format to introduce students to broader techniques and applications of data management, building on their existing background and experiences with data management tools. Students will read the assigned material prior to the week's classes as preparation for engaging in an in-class discussion to be lead by a student presenter. Students are expected to pick one topic area and present the two papers chosen for that area in two 50 minute lectures for Wednesday and Friday class. To get the most out of the material, my suggestion is that presentations should be done in the style of tutorials at conferences, that is they should include both introductory material on the topic and summarize the contributions of the assigned papers on the topic, rather than purely focus on the papers. Given 75 minute lecture slots, the remaining 25 minutes will be a class discussion and follow-up material by the instructor linking the week's materials to the remaining topics in the syllabus.

For homework assignments, students will write two short papers (3-6 pages) on topics chosen by the instructor based on the areas covered in the course. These topics will either be a brief literature survey, a methods or systems comparison, or an experiment and benchmarking oriented topic. We'll announce the topics shortly as the course gets underway.

This year, the course project will aim at aggregating group projects to produce an open genomics database. This database will be deployed onto cluster resources available in the Data Management Systems Lab, and IDIES. We've divided the set of tasks involved in this effort into three areas (see below), and included a list of topics in each area on the project page. Students should form groups of 2-3, and register their groups on our sign-up form by September 18, 2013. Groups should indicate two project area preferences based on interests. In addition to the main thrust of their individual topic, project groups will also be required to interact and integrate their efforts with other groups during the semester as part of a class-wide effort. We will conduct an end-of-semester, end-to-end demonstration of the genomics database during the last week of class to genomics researchers on the JHU Homewood and School of Medicine campuses.

Syllabus

Week Topic
1. Course introduction and organization.
2. Big Data Systems introduction (Hadoop).
3. Interactive data exploration (Dremel/Impala).
4. Scalable analysis and introductory machine learning on Hadoop.
5. Applications, part i: introduction to sequence analysis.
6. Analytics workloads in large web companies.
7. Graph data processing (GraphLab).
8. Main-memory and resilient processing (Spark).
9. Mid-semester demonstrations.
10. Distributed transactional storage.
11. Applications part ii: advanced sequence analysis.
12. Introduction to scalable convex optimization.
13. Parallel and randomized matrix algorithms.
14. Final project demonstrations.
